Bridging Landscapes: Lessons from Sri Lanka for a Greener, Resilient Senegal

  • Medhavi Kumarathunga
  • April 15, 2025
  • 0 Comments

As the sole representative of Sri Lanka at the “Building Resilient Landscapes” training program in Senegal, I am honored to bring not only technical knowledge but also the deeply rooted agricultural wisdom of Sri Lanka—known for centuries as “The Granary of the East.”

Sri Lanka and Senegal may appear vastly different in geography, but both countries face similar environmental challenges—unpredictable rainfall, land degradation, and long dry spells. Yet, Sri Lanka has used innovation and tradition to transform arid lands into green productivity zones—lessons Senegal can adopt for sustainable agricultural futures.

Key Sustainable Strategies from Sri Lanka

  • Jackfruit: A climate-resilient, multi-purpose crop that can provide food, timber, and shade, ideal for Senegal’s drylands.
  • Smart Irrigation: Like Sri Lanka’s ancient wewas (reservoirs), Senegal can green canal corridors with crops like palm oil and sugarcane.
  • Integrated Crop Zones: Planting coconut, guava, and fruit trees around rice fields improves biodiversity and reduces erosion.
  • Tuber Crops: Sweet potatoes and plantains thrive in low-water conditions, boosting nutrition and income in rural areas.
  • Hill Farming: Short highland cultivation zones can grow crops like chilies, cabbage, and watermelon effectively.
  • Livestock Integration: Using cattle and goats near crops maximizes land productivity and supports organic fertilization.
  • Transitional Crops: Between paddy seasons, Sri Lankan farmers grow legumes and nuts to keep soils fertile and generate income.
  • Agri-Business Units: Organizing farmers into micro-enterprises creates autonomy, empowers women and youth, and improves marketing reach.

“Let us sow together not just seeds of crops—but seeds of transformation.”

This journey is about more than agriculture—it’s about resilience, collaboration, and building food security from the ground up. As Sri Lanka shares its strategies with Senegal, a bridge of green innovation and mutual learning is being built across continents.

By adapting proven practices with respect for local ecosystems, both nations can cultivate a more sustainable, food-secure future—together.
